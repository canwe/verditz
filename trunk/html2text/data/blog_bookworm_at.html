[OpenOffice] Writer to Wiki

OpenOffice 2.3 Writer comes with a Export to Mediawiki feature. Since OpenOffice Documents are standardized XML Documents, implementing such an export filter usually means that you just write some kind of stylesheet that transforms the XML to something else. XSLT might be a good candidate for a transformation language, since it was made exactly for this type of task.

For example, If you want to export to some other Format than Mediawiki (e.g. Markdown) , you might want to follow Dmitri Popov’s advice, and look at the source of the OpenOffice2UniWakka export filter. There, you will find a XSLT Stylesheet that you can modify to build your own export filter.

When was it that Google shot itself into it's most reliable foot?

It took a while until I noticed this, but Google apparently decided to update its search index a while ago, with lots of stuff that is not accessible on the internet!

Just try and search for something like text classification bayes network and you will find lots of results that are marked as PDF or Postscript documents. But not all of these documents are really of that file type, and if you try to read them, you will often find yourself on some proprietary IEEE/ACM/Whatever "register and become a member now" page, because the document you found is restricted to (often paying) members of these services. It seems that Google bought itself the license to go where we cannot go, and index stuff that we cannot view.

I have really no idea what they were smoking. We’re not even talking about Google Scholar here, but plain old search.

I hope this is some ridiculous mistake, or some really clever strategy I do not yet understand. But until they fixed this -what I call a- bug, every other search engine has a huge advantage over Google now: They only show me stuff that I can actually view.

Tuesday, September 11, 2007

What programmers fear more than death

Erik Naggum on comp.lang.lisp:

"Why is it that programmers always seem to think that the rest of the world is stupid?"

Because they are autodidacts. The main purpose of higher education and making all the smartest kids from one school come together with all the smartest kids from other schools, recursively, is to show every smart kid everywhere that they are not the smartest kid around, that no matter how smart they are, they are not equally smart at everything even though they were just that to begin with, and there will therefore always be smarter kids, if nothing else, than at something other than they are smart at.

If you take a smart kid out of this system, reward him with lots of money that he could never make otherwise, reward him with control over machines that journalists are morbidly afraid of and make the entire population fear second-hand, and prevent him from ever meeting smarter people than himself, he will have no recourse but to believe that he /is/ smarter than everybody else. Educate him properly and force him to reach the point of intellectual exhaustion and failure where there is no other route to success than to ask for help, and he will gain a profound respect for other people.

Many programmers act like they are morbidly afraid of being discovered to be less smart than they think they are, and many of them respond with extreme hostility on Usenet precisely because they get a glimpse of their own limitations. To people whose entire life has been about being in control, loss of control is actually a very good reason to panic.

Erik Naggum | Oslo, Norway 2004-026

Act from reason, and failure makes you rethink and study harder. Act from faith, and failure makes you blame someone and push harder.
